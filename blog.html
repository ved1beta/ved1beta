MXFP4, End-to-End: How a 4-bit Recipe Travels from pytorch/ao to Axolotl

Three lines of YAML. A year of plumbing. The trace between them.
by Ved · systems engineer at Axolotl AI
github / ved1beta · x / @ant_vedaya

Three lines in a YAML file:

qat:
  activation_dtype: mxfp4
  weight_dtype:     mxfp4
  group_size:       32

A user drops these into an Axolotl config, runs axolotl train, and a Llama starts training with simulated 4-bit numerics on a consumer GPU. No custom kernels. No hand-rolled quantization loop. Just three lines, and everything downstream of them works.

Getting to those three lines took about a year of work across three different codebases. This post is the trace of what happens between them and the GPU — and what I learned while shipping PRs into each layer of the stack.

I’m a systems engineer at Axolotl. Over the past year I’ve contributed to pytorch/ao (the PyTorch-native quantization library), bitsandbytes (the 8-bit optimizer and 4-bit quantization library that almost every QLoRA workflow depends on), and Axolotl itself. MXFP4 QAT is one of the threads that passes through all three. This is the plumbing post I wish had existed when I started.

§ 01What MXFP4 actually is

MXFP4 is a 4-bit floating-point format standardized by the Open Compute Project as part of the microscaling (MX) family. Three things matter about it:

  • Block size 32. Every 32 consecutive values in a tensor share one scale factor. That’s what makes it “microscaling” — scales are local to small blocks, not global to a whole tensor.
  • E2M1 element format. Each value is 1 sign bit, 2 exponent bits, 1 mantissa bit. That gives you exactly 7 positive representable values — 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0, 6.0 — plus their negatives and zero. 16 total values per element, 4 bits.
  • UE8M0 shared scale. The block-level scale is an 8-bit unsigned exponent with no mantissa — essentially a power-of-2 multiplier. Cheap to store, cheap to multiply.

The combination is engineered for hardware. Blackwell has native MXFP4 tensor cores. Hopper doesn’t, but the format is emulable there with minor overhead. Ampere and earlier emulate it more expensively but can still use it for QAT, where the numerics matter but raw throughput doesn’t.

For training, the trick is fake quantization: you don’t actually compute in MXFP4. You keep weights and activations in BF16, but before every matmul you quantize-then-dequantize them through the MXFP4 grid, which simulates the numerical behavior without needing a production 4-bit matmul kernel. The model learns to be robust to the quantization noise.

This fake-quantization trick is the single design decision that makes the entire training-to-serving pipeline possible across three libraries. Hold onto it — it comes back at the end.

§ 02Layer 1 — pytorch/ao as the primitive

pytorch/ao is the PyTorch-native model optimization library. The TorchAO paper frames its mission as “connecting an otherwise fragmented space” — a unified representation of low-precision numerics that flows from pre-training through fine-tuning to serving. MXFP4 is one of the tensor types in that unified representation.

At the API level, QAT with MXFP4 in pytorch/ao looks like this:

# wrap the model’s Linear layers with fake-quant subclasses
quantize_(model, QATConfig(mx_config, step="prepare"))

# ... train as normal ...

# swap fake-quant subclasses for real MX tensors
quantize_(model, QATConfig(mx_config, step="convert"))

Under the hood, MXFP4 is a PyTorch tensor subclass. The subclass intercepts matmul and performs fake quantization along the path; gradients flow back through a straight-through estimator. The elegance is that the optimizer, the dataloader, and the loss function have no idea they’re training a quantized model.

PR: add support for MXFP8 and MXFP4 QAT pytorch/ao#3644

Before this PR, torchao.prototype.qat had an NVFP4 fake-quantization path but no MX-family equivalent. MXFP4 and MXFP8 existed only in the inference workflow. If you wanted QAT with an MX format, you had to wire up the tensor subclass yourself.

The PR adds MXFakeQuantizeConfig and MXFakeQuantizedLinear in a new mx.py, structurally mirroring the NVFP4 file next to it. It routes MXFP4, MXFP8 E4M3, and MXFP8 E5M2 through the existing quantize_ API with block size 32.

The first version I submitted got two things wrong that I didn’t see until review. The smaller: I’d called the element-dtype field elem_dtype; the rest of ao calls it dtype. The larger: my initial MXFakeQuantizeConfig stored scaling_mode and kernel_preference as hardcoded constants instead of inheriting from the base config. It worked, but it wasn’t a proper citizen of ao’s config hierarchy. The rewrite — “scaling_mode and kernel_preference pass from base” — is where the config stopped being a special case and started composing with the library.

The other foundational catch was a missing equivalence test between QAT prepare and PTQ forward. The resulting test_quantize_api_mx asserts target_prepare_sqnr=float("inf") — QAT and PTQ forward must match exactly, because they share kernels. That test is what lets Axolotl downstream trust that step="prepare" and step="convert" produce numerically compatible outputs.

§ 03Layer 2 — bitsandbytes and the optimizer problem

Quantization discussions focus on weights and activations. But a transformer being trained has a third large memory consumer: optimizer state. For Adam on N parameters, you carry roughly 2N floats of momentum and variance, plus N floats of parameters, plus N of gradients. 4× the parameter count in memory during training.

bitsandbytes’ 8-bit optimizers compress momentum and variance from FP32 to INT8 with block-wise dequantization — roughly 4× less optimizer memory. Combined with QLoRA-style 4-bit weights, this is how finetuning 70B models on consumer GPUs became possible.

But there’s a seam.

When you add FSDP into the mix, optimizer state has to be split across ranks. FSDP’s collectives expect one tensor per parameter, shaped like that parameter. bitsandbytes’ 8-bit optimizer stored more than that per parameter. The failure has been known since 2022 — issue #89, 8-bit optimizers don’t work with FSDP. For years the workaround was to not combine the two.

PR: bug: fix 8bitoptim support with fsdp bnb#1840 · v0.49.2

The concrete path is Optimizer8bit.state_dict(). For every parameter, it wrote a flat dict containing everything that parameter’s optimizer needed: state1, state2 (momentum and variance), plus absmax1, absmax2, and related metadata.

FSDP’s full_optim_state_dict walks that structure across ranks, descends one level, and treats every tensor it finds as something to gather as if it shards like the parameter. state1 and state2 do. absmax1 doesn’t — it’s shaped by the block structure of the quantized buffer, not the parameter. FSDP tries to gather it, the shapes don’t line up across ranks, and you get RuntimeError: output tensor must have the same type as input tensor from deep inside NCCL.

The fix is a nesting trick. state_dict() now wraps all quantization-specific tensors in a sub-dict under a new constant, _FSDP_WRAPPED_QUANT_STATE_KEY. FSDP’s gather walks the outer dict, sees one value that’s itself a dict, and because the gather only descends one level, it ignores the sub-dict’s contents. load_state_dict() transparently unwraps on the way back in.

What’s non-obvious about the fix is that the answer isn’t “cast something differently.” The fix is about the shape of the data structure, not its contents. The tensors are the same before and after the PR; only their nesting depth in the state dict changes. That’s the kind of fix that’s easy to miss because you’re looking for a type bug — you read “output tensor must have the same type as input tensor” and your brain fixates on dtype.

This is not a bitsandbytes bug. It’s not an FSDP bug either. The bug lives at the interface. This is most of what systems work is: reading two libraries neither of whose authors were imagining the other.

Issue #89 closed after four open years.

§ 04Layer 3 — Axolotl as the integration point

Axolotl is an open-source fine-tuning framework. Users write a YAML config; Axolotl configures the model, the dataset, the trainer, and runs. It’s the user-facing layer — MXFP4 QAT support is what lets pytorch/ao’s primitive and bitsandbytes’ fixed optimizer actually reach a practitioner.

PR: add: support mxfp4 axo axolotl#3375

The change is small by line count and deliberately so. A new mxfp4 member on TorchAOQuantDType. Validation in validate_ao_dtype accepting the string "mxfp4". A guarded from torchao.prototype.qat import MXFakeQuantizeConfig (try/except so Axolotl still loads against older ao builds). A branch in get_quantization_config that instantiates the config with float4_e2m1fn_x2 and enforces block_size=32. Plus an example at examples/llama-3/3b-qat-mxfp4.yaml, tuned to fit in 32 GB of VRAM.

The harder-than-expected part was ordering. The PR blocks on pytorch/ao#3644 — the config literally doesn’t exist until that merges. The natural instinct was to vendor the config into Axolotl; I didn’t, for the reasons below.

Two review comments changed the implementation. A review bot caught that I’d written "mxfp" in the reverse map where I meant "mxfp4" — one character short. And an earlier version of the example YAML wasn’t actually using QAT: it quantized, but not during training. The rewrite added the proper qat: block; a maintainer then did the final VRAM-tuning commit that made the example runnable on a single consumer GPU.

Once this lands, the full chain closes. The user writes three lines of YAML. Axolotl parses them into a pytorch/ao config. pytorch/ao wraps the model’s Linear layers with MXFP4 fake-quant tensor subclasses. FSDP shards the model across GPUs, and bitsandbytes’ (now-fixed) 8-bit optimizer shards the optimizer state alongside it. Training runs. A QAT checkpoint drops out the other end, ready to be quantized and served through vLLM or SGLang.

§ 05What the seams taught me

1 — Upstream-first is slower, and always worth it.

The fastest path was to vendor quantization logic directly into Axolotl. A day of work. Instead it took weeks because the primitive had to land in pytorch/ao first, get reviewed, get released, and only then get integrated downstream. But the same primitive now flows into every framework that adopts torchao. The version of me that wanted to ship fast was optimizing for the wrong time horizon.

2 — The hardest bugs live at the interfaces.

The bitsandbytes FSDP fix was not a bug inside bitsandbytes. It was a bug about bitsandbytes’ implicit contract with FSDP. This pattern repeats everywhere. Quantization × distributed training. Training × serving. Eager × compile. The interesting bugs are almost never inside one library; they’re between two.

3 — “Fake” quantization is why the stack can exist at all.

If QAT required production 4-bit matmul kernels to work, we’d not have a three-library stack. Each library would need to ship its own low-precision compute path. Instead, because fake quantization keeps values in BF16 and simulates the numerics, only pytorch/ao has to understand MXFP4. Everyone downstream can stay oblivious and still be correct. When you see a stack that cleanly layers across three libraries, look for the equivalent decision — there’s usually one choice, made early, that bought everyone else the right to ignore each other.
